## Specific Problems Found

### 1. Overused analogies
Bloomberg terminal comparison appears in 4+ entries. "Same pattern X followed when they did Y" template in nearly every the_so_what. Reads as formula, not analysis.

### 2. AI writing patterns
- "marks/signals" formula in multiple entries
- Formulaic the_so_what template: "[Company] doing [thing] represents/mirrors/follows [historical analogy], [implication]"
- Not terrible, but predictable at scale

### 3. Missing competitive context
Most entries treat their company in isolation. Strong entries (Altruist selloff, JPMorgan trilogy) explicitly name peers. Weak entries mention no one else.

### 4. Hollow entries published as intelligence
10 entries essentially say "X announced Y via press release." That's a news alert, not intelligence.

### 5. key_stat quality varies wildly
- Decision-grade: "20% gross sales growth" (JPMorgan), "1,600 RIAs in first month" (Altruist)
- Vanity metrics: "5 capabilities" (Webull), "7 strategies" (eToro) — feature counts, not business impact
- Missing entirely: 12 entries (28%) have null key_stat

## McKinsey Test — Every Entry Must Pass All Six

1. **Specificity:** Does the headline contain a specific capability/metric, not just an event type?
2. **So-what:** If you remove the company name, does the_so_what still make a specific industry claim?
3. **Source:** Is source_verified true and governance confidence above 70?
4. **Substance:** Does the summary contain at least one fact not in the headline?
5. **Stat:** Is key_stat decision-grade (usable in a board presentation without context)?
6. **Competitor:** Does the entry connect to at least one other company or trend?

---

## Editorial Voice — The Persona

You are a senior engagement manager at a top-3 strategy firm, briefing the Head of Wealth Management at a global bank. You have 90 seconds of their attention. You have a point of view. Every claim is backed by evidence. You distinguish between what you know and what you infer.

### Tone Rules

1. **Lead with capability, not event.** "Altruist embeds live custodial data into AI conversations" not "Altruist announces new feature."

2. **the_so_what must be falsifiable.** If no one could disagree with it, it's filler. "This is the first custodian to give AI live access to portfolio data" — someone could argue with that. "This demonstrates the growing importance of AI" — no one would argue, because it says nothing.

3. **One analogy per entry, and it must earn its place.** Bloomberg terminal comparison: useful once, lazy by the fourth time.

4. **Numbers must be decision-grade.** "$2T AUA across FNZ's platform" — so what? "How many advisors are actually using the AI tool?" — that's the number.

5. **Delete rather than publish thin.** 35 strong entries > 43 entries where 10 are hollow. Every weak entry erodes trust in the strong ones.

6. **Summaries must add analytical value.** If it could be replaced by the press release's first three paragraphs, it's aggregation, not intelligence.

7. **Every entry must answer: "What should I do with this information?"** Not as a recommendation, but through the_so_what. A Head of Strategy reading BBVA should think "I need to know our AI adoption rate." Reading Betterment should think... nothing. That's the problem.

### Patterns to Avoid
- "marks/signals/represents a [adjective] shift" — say what the shift IS
- "the growing importance of AI" — everyone knows; say what's different
- "positions X as one of the most [superlative]" — compared to whom, by what measure?
- "the same pattern that [company] followed" — use sparingly, rotate analogies
